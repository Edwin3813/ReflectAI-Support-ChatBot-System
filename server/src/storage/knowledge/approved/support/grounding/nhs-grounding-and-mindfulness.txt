Grounding-style support focuses on bringing attention back to the present moment when thoughts feel overwhelming, distressing, or hard to slow down.

A practical way to do this is to gently notice what is happening right now, such as breathing, sounds, physical sensations, or details in the immediate environment.

Supportive grounding and mindfulness ideas may include:
- paying attention to the sensation of breathing
- noticing sounds, body sensations, or nearby objects
- bringing attention back whenever the mind wanders
- using a simple sensory exercise to reconnect with the present
- keeping the approach gentle rather than forcing calm

A grounding exercise can be helpful when someone feels overwhelmed and needs a simple, immediate step. One example often used in NHS settings is a sensory check-in, such as noticing things you can see, hear, or feel around you.

Grounding support content should avoid sounding clinical or prescriptive. It should offer calm, practical steps and make clear that different techniques work for different people.
